Five Signals to Watch — May, 2026
Breaking
Women's Reservation Bill defeated — first defeat of a constitutional amendment in the current Parliament
What moved

The Constitution (131st Amendment) Bill — providing 33% reservation for women in Parliament and state assemblies — was defeated in the Lok Sabha on April 17, 2026, with 298 votes in favour and 230 against, falling short of the required two-thirds majority. The BJP linked implementation to a simultaneous expansion of the Lok Sabha and a fresh delimitation exercise. The opposition held firm. It is the first constitutional amendment to be blocked under the current government. The 2023 Nari Shakti Vandan Act remains law, but implementation is now tied to a political timeline that extends beyond one election cycle.

What to do

For organisations working on governance or political participation: the gap between the 2023 Act and its implementation is now documented at the apex level. Civil society evidence on what delayed implementation costs women politically is directly relevant to the parliamentary record. For bilateral donors, this signals continued uncertainty around legal quotas — elevating the importance of civil society-led demand-side programming.

Source: Lok Sabha records, April 17, 2026; NUS Institute of South Asian Studies, April 21, 2026
Read Carefully
Gender Budget 2026-27 crosses Rs 5 lakh crore — but 72.6% sits in Part B
What moved

India's Gender Budget 2026-27 crossed Rs 5 lakh crore — 9.37% of total Union Budget expenditure, the highest share in two decades. But 72.6% of that sits in Part B: schemes where 30-99% of allocation goes to women, making outcome tracking difficult. Safety and crisis-response services remain under 1% of the total gender budget. ASHA and ANM workers received no structural change in classification or pay. LPG connections remain in Part A (100% women-specific), a classification analysts have flagged as reinforcing rather than transforming gender roles.

What to do

Use the 9.37% figure carefully — it overstates transformation. The more useful advocacy number is under-1% for safety and crisis-response. Proposals should cite the Part A vs Part B breakdown when making adequacy arguments. For care economy work, the Economic Survey 2025-26 unpaid care acknowledgment remains the strongest policy peg.

Source: Gender Budget Statement 2026-27, Ministry of Finance; Population Foundation of India budget analysis, February 2026
Watch This
SHE Marts: guidelines still undefined, consultations now live in three states
What moved

Three months after Budget 2026-27 announced SHE Marts, implementation guidelines remain unpublished. Financing mechanisms are undefined. Income protection is absent from any draft guidance. State NRLM teams in Rajasthan, UP, and now Maharashtra are informally consulting SHG federations. Risk remains concentrated on federation women. The design is still live and contestable — but the window is narrowing.

What to do

If your programme has field presence in any of these states: document what informal guidance your NRLM counterpart has received. That documentation is evidence for the formal consultation process. Organisations with SHG federation partnerships should begin a risk assessment on income continuity and liability architecture now — before women's groups commit.

Source: Union Budget 2026-27; NRLM civil society monitoring, March–May 2026
Data Alert
MGNREGS: two corrections needed in every active donor document
What moved

Two simultaneous MGNREGS updates are active and most organisations have caught neither. First: women's share corrected from 33.6% to 46.5% in the Gender Budget Statement 2025-26. Second: daily wages revised from Rs 349 to Rs 370 effective April 1, 2026. Any document produced after April 2026 citing either pre-correction figure carries a data error that technical reviewers will flag.

What to do

One-hour audit: (1) Replace 33.6% with 46.5% — cite Gender Budget Statement 2025-26. (2) Replace Rs 349/day with Rs 370/day — effective April 1, 2026. Two corrections, significant credibility protection.

Source: Gender Budget Statement 2025-26, Ministry of Finance; MoRD wage revision notification, March 2026
Policy Gap
ASHA and ANM workers: Budget 2026 silence opens an advocacy moment
What moved

Budget 2026-27 made no change to ASHA worker classification as volunteers, no increase in base honoraria, and no movement on the pending Supreme Court petition on minimum wage recognition. Nearly one million ASHAs continue to be paid Rs 2,000–5,000 in fixed honoraria — structurally below minimum wage in every state. The new Labour Codes extended social security to platform and gig workers but did not include ASHA workers, despite their structural similarity to unorganised workers performing essential public services.

What to do

For health systems organisations: Budget silence is itself evidence for advocacy documents. The gap between Labour Codes' stated coverage of unorganised workers and non-inclusion of ASHAs is a specific, citable legal inconsistency. For proposal writers: "community health workers operating without employment protections" now has Budget 2026 as an implicit source.

Source: Population Foundation of India, Budget 2026 analysis; Labour Codes consolidation, November 2025; Parliamentary Standing Committee on Health, 2025

First in South Asia
Regional Firsts
Policies that changed the playbook
South Asia has produced some of the world's most quietly radical policy innovations on gender and governance. These are the ones worth knowing — not just as history, but as arguments.
🇱🇰
Sri Lanka
The world's first elected female head of government — in 1960
Sirimavo Bandaranaike became the world's first female Prime Minister when she won the 1960 election in then-Ceylon — predating Margaret Thatcher by nearly twenty years. She served three non-consecutive terms. Her daughter Chandrika later became Sri Lanka's first female President, the only time in history that a female Prime Minister directly succeeded another. The lesson for today's policymakers: South Asia did not wait for the West to show the way.
Elected July 21, 1960 · Guinness World Record holder
🇳🇵
Nepal
First country in Asia to constitutionally mandate 33% women in parliament — at every level
Nepal's 2015 Constitution guarantees at least one-third women's participation in the federal parliament, all provincial assemblies, and local government — the most comprehensive gender quota embedded in a constitution anywhere in Asia. The World Bank's Women, Business and Law Index 2024 gives Nepal the highest score in South Asia for closing the gender gap. Nepal now ranks first in South Asia for women's parliamentary representation, above the global average of 25%.
Constitution of Nepal, 2015 · World Bank WBL Index 2024
🇮🇳
India — Kerala
26 weeks of paid maternity leave: among the most generous in Asia when enacted
When India's Maternity Benefit (Amendment) Act 2017 extended paid maternity leave from 12 weeks to 26 weeks, it placed India among the most progressive countries in Asia on this measure — ahead of Japan (16 weeks), Singapore (16 weeks), and the OECD average. The amendment also introduced a provision for mothers to work from home post-leave, and extended protections to mothers adopting children under three months. Kerala's consistent implementation and Kudumbashree's SHG infrastructure have made this more than a paper provision in the state.
Maternity Benefit (Amendment) Act, 2017 · Among top globally when enacted
