01
What happened, in order
January 20, 2025
Executive order freezes all US foreign aid for 90 days
Trump's first-day executive order paused most USAID-supported projects worldwide. India's programmes received stop-work notices within days. Organisations that had multi-year grant agreements were told to halt activities except staff salary disbursements.
January 24, 2025
India's Mitr clinics shut immediately
India's first dedicated transgender healthcare clinics in Hyderabad, Thane, and Pune — serving nearly 5,000 registered patients — ceased operations on receiving USAID freeze notices. Clinics had been running since 2021, implemented by Johns Hopkins University under PEPFAR funding.
March 10, 2025
83% of USAID programmes formally cancelled
Secretary of State Rubio confirmed approximately 5,200 contracts terminated — 83% of USAID's global portfolio. In India, this meant the end of TB support programmes including "Breaking the Barriers" (Karnataka Health Promotion Trust), HIV/AIDS programmes under PEPFAR, and women's economic empowerment work under WOHLA.
July 1, 2025
USAID formally ceases to exist
The agency was dissolved into the State Department. Approximately 250 staff transferred over — from a workforce that previously numbered over 10,000. Remaining programmes are administered with reduced capacity under State Department management. The agency is legally still in existence as only Congress can formally abolish it, but operationally it is gone.
July 18, 2025
Congress claws back $9 billion in foreign assistance
US House Republicans approved the rescissions package, cutting $8.3 billion from USAID and foreign assistance. PEPFAR received a partial reprieve of $400 million after moderate Republicans objected. The cuts reduce disbursements from 2026 onward, compounding the 2025 programme cancellations.
April 2026 — where we are now
Gap-filling is partial, uneven, and politically constrained
GIZ, FCDO, Sida, and Indian foundations are all active in the conversation about absorbing USAID-vacated programming. None have matched the scale or political breadth of what USAID funded. The gap in India's gender sector remains real, specific, and in many areas unaddressed.

03
What it means — by sector
Critical gap
TB programme technical assistance — terminated
The iDEFEAT TB project (The Union, $2.92M) provided technical support to India's National TB Elimination Programme — new drug access, treatment regimen support, capacity building for rural health workers. This is now gone. India's TB elimination target of 2025 was already delayed. The gender dimension of TB — women's distinct access barriers to diagnosis and treatment — was specifically tracked under this programme.
Critical gap
Transgender healthcare — 5,000 patients without services
Mitr clinics in Hyderabad, Thane, and Pune shut January 24, 2025. 5,000 registered patients lost access to HIV prevention, hormone therapy, mental health support, and legal aid. Telangana State AIDS Control Society has offered partial funding — not sufficient to restore full capacity. The Lancet documented this as a healthcare crisis in May 2025.
Watch
HIV/AIDS services — PEPFAR funding partially retained
Congressional intervention in July 2025 saved $400M of PEPFAR globally. However, the Trump administration's executive orders against "gender ideology" mean HIV programmes for sex workers and MSM populations — highest-risk groups — face content restrictions even where funding continues. HIV programmes for women in sex work in India are in a grey zone.
Watch
Maternal health — MOMENTUM programme ended
USAID's MOMENTUM programme, which trained Indian doctors on C-section delivery to reduce maternal mortality, has been shut down. Grassroots-level NGOs that received sub-grants from MOMENTUM's implementing partners have lost funding mid-programme. No government scheme has announced equivalent coverage.
What organisations in health and GBV should do now
1
Map your current programme's dependency on any implementing partner that was previously USAID-funded. If you were a sub-grantee, your anchor funder's revenue has contracted — budget conversations are happening now.
2
Frame your TB or HIV work using NHM language, not USAID project language. The Indian government is the remaining funder of record and their vocabulary is what gets you through the door.
3
The Nirbhaya Fund civil society window (Rs 1,631 crore unreleased) is the most proximate domestic alternative for GBV-adjacent programming. Position before the formal call opens.
Critical gap
Women's economic empowerment for marginalised groups
WOHLA and similar programmes — training low-income and rural women in technology, budgeting, and reproductive health access — are discontinued. These are the populations that bilaterals and Indian foundations find hardest to fund due to political sensitivity. USAID was one of few funders willing to work explicitly in these spaces.
Watch
SHG programmes — USAID's financial inclusion work withdraws
USAID had India-specific financial inclusion programming supporting SHG-linked women's enterprise. This coincides exactly with the SHE Marts rollout — a scheme pushing SHG federations into retail without defined financing. The policy ambition has increased; the implementation support has shrunk.
Opening
NSFI 2025-30 creates domestic policy hook
The National Strategy for Financial Inclusion 2025-30 names gender-sensitive financial inclusion for the first time in an RBI-anchored document. This is the strongest available policy alignment tool for women's economic empowerment programmes seeking GIZ or FCDO funding in the post-USAID landscape.
Opening
Indian foundations moving into the space
Rohini Nilekani Philanthropies, Tata Trusts, and Azim Premji Philanthropic Initiatives are all actively reviewing whether to expand livelihoods portfolios in response to USAID withdrawal. The constraint: they are less willing to fund politically sensitive categories. Programming that can be framed in non-contentious language has the best chance.
What organisations in livelihoods should do now
1
Reframe your programme narrative using NSFI 2025-30 and Economic Survey 2025-26 language (unpaid care work acknowledgment). These are now official government documents — citing them positions you as policy-aligned, not donor-dependent.
2
The UN Women India CSO grants round for UP and Rajasthan livelihoods work closes April 30, 2026. If your programme has field presence in either state, this is the most immediate funding window available.
3
Approach GIZ India with a specific ask: the GSDP Gender Study findings from March 2025 show a gap between their gender strategy and field implementation. Your evidence base is exactly what they need to justify expanded programming.
Critical gap
CDC's technical assistance to India is withdrawing
The US CDC worked with India's Ministry of Health and Family Welfare on gender-disaggregated data collection, protocol monitoring, and health systems analysis. "If these consultants go away, India's big healthcare programmes like TB elimination and HIV control may suffer because they require good technical expertise at central and state levels," The Wire reported in March 2025.
Critical gap
Gender-disaggregated surveillance — no domestic equivalent
USAID funded specific tracking of gender outcomes in health programmes. India currently has no domestic surveillance mechanism that tracks the gender dimension of TB incidence, HIV risk, and maternal mortality at the granularity that USAID-funded programmes maintained. The data gap will widen over time.
Context
MGNREGS correction — a cautionary example
The correction of women's MGNREGS share from 33.6% to 46.5% — which most organisations haven't caught yet — illustrates the systemic problem: when the technical capacity to track gender data is under-resourced, errors compound silently. The loss of USAID's data infrastructure will produce more of these gaps, less visibly.
Opening
Gender Budget Tracker — genuine market gap
With USAID's technical teams gone, there is no longer a systematic external monitor of India's gender budgeting implementation. Civil society organisations that can produce credible, timely analysis of gender budget utilisation are filling a gap that used to be occupied by USAID-funded research. This is precisely what Southward Collective's Gender Budget Tracker is designed to do.
What organisations in research and M&E should do now
1
Audit every gender data point in your current active documents. If it came from a USAID-funded research product or indicator framework, verify whether it is still being updated. Many are not.
2
Bilateral donors — GIZ, FCDO, Sida — are actively looking for India-based research organisations to fill the data function USAID previously anchored. A proposal to establish a gender outcomes monitoring function is fundable right now in a way it wasn't two years ago.
3
The Parliamentary Standing Committee on WCD will increasingly rely on NGO-sourced evidence as government data systems absorb the CDC withdrawal. Submitting evidence to parliamentary committees is now more influential than it was before.

Who is actually stepping in — and what they will and will not fund. This is the intelligence that most sector organisations don't have systematically.

GIZ India
German bilateral, via GSDP
Expanding gender programming through the Indo-German Green and Sustainable Development Partnership. New Gender Strategy 2025-2029 launched February 2025. Active in green skills for women, rural livelihoods, and solar energy gender mainstreaming. Will fund: livelihoods, skills, climate-gender nexus. Will not fund at scale: health delivery, HIV/AIDS, transgender-specific work.
Actively expanding
FCDO India
UK bilateral
Has a published feminist foreign policy framework. India Country Development Partnership Summary due for update mid-2026 — watch this for portfolio shifts. Active on girls' education and women's economic empowerment. Will fund: education, economic empowerment, governance. Constrained by own 0.5% ODA cap for 2025-26.
Partial coverage
Indian Foundations
Rohini Nilekani, Tata, Azim Premji
Fastest-moving segment. All three are reviewing India livelihoods and health portfolio expansion. Decision-making is faster than bilaterals. Will fund: livelihoods, health systems, research, advocacy. Will not fund: politically sensitive categories (sex work, transgender-specific care, anything that reads as "gender ideology" to corporate trustees).
Selectively expanding
State Governments
SACS, NHM state units
Filling gaps partially and at reduced scale. Telangana SACS offered partial Mitr clinic funding. Pattern repeating across USAID-terminated programmes. Represents a structural shift from NGO delivery toward government-adjacent delivery. Coverage is geographically uneven — southern states more responsive than northern.
Partial only
Gates Foundation India
Philanthropy
Active on health systems and nutrition. India portfolio has historically overlapped with USAID in TB and maternal health. Will fund: evidence-based health programming with strong M&E. Will not fund: advocacy, community organising, anything that looks like rights-based work without an outcomes frame.
Selective
Sida (Sweden)
Swedish bilateral
Committed to 0.7% ODA target. One of few bilaterals willing to fund rights-based work explicitly. India is not a priority country for Sida's direct programming but they fund Swedish NGOs (like Forum Syd) working in India. An indirect route, but available for organisations with Swedish partner connections.
Indirect only
04
What to do with this — by role
1
Audit your funding chain for USAID exposure
If any of your funding comes through an anchor implementer — IPE Global, Palladium, FHI 360, Johns Hopkins — ask them directly whether their contracts were USAID-funded and what the status is. Sub-grantee exposure often isn't obvious until the anchor's revenue contracts.
2
Shift your programme narrative language now
Documents that use USAID-era language ("gender equality programming," "PEPFAR-aligned") need to be rewritten in domestic policy terms. The NSFI 2025-30, Economic Survey 2025-26 unpaid care acknowledgment, and NHM framing are your new anchor citations.
3
Check the April 30 UN Women window
UN Women India's open CSO grants round for gender-responsive livelihoods in UP and Rajasthan closes April 30, 2026. If your programme has field presence in either state, this is the most immediately accessible funding currently open in the sector.
1
Read the FCDO Development Tracker for India now
Every active FCDO-funded India project is listed publicly with budget, duration, and thematic focus. Reading this tells you what language FCDO is using, what's ending, and where the call for new partners will likely open. The India Country Development Partnership Summary is due for update mid-2026.
2
Reframe your donor diversification strategy
Competition for GIZ, FCDO, Gates, and Indian foundation funding has intensified significantly as USAID-dependent organisations pivot. The organisations that will succeed are those that can demonstrate alignment with the funder's current stated priorities — not just the funder's historical areas.
3
The gap creates a case for unrestricted funding
Approach Indian foundations with a specific ask for unrestricted operating support, framed as: "the organisations filling the USAID gap need flexibility to respond to what the sector actually needs, not just what a project proposal committed to two years ago." This framing works right now in a way it rarely does.
1
Map the India portfolio against the USAID gap
The three areas most at risk from USAID withdrawal in India's gender sector are: TB/HIV gender-specific programming, transgender and marginalised community health, and gender-disaggregated data surveillance. Ask internally which of these your programme is positioned to absorb and which require new mechanisms.
2
Commission a landscape scan before your next programme design
Policy assumptions baked into programme proposals in 2023-24 are now outdated. The FCRA Amendment 2026, the SHE Marts rollout, the MGNREGS correction, and the USAID withdrawal together constitute a materially different operating environment. A fresh landscape scan is not optional for the next design cycle.
3
Note the GIZ Gender Strategy 2025-2029 alignment opportunity
GIZ's new gender strategy launched February 2025 with explicit India focus. Joint programming with GIZ through the GSDP frame could enable cost-sharing on gender mainstreaming work that neither bilateral has the budget to anchor alone.
1
Verify every data point from USAID-funded research products
USAID funded specific gender data collection in India — TB incidence by gender, HIV risk disaggregation, maternal mortality tracking. Any of these products published before July 2025 may no longer have an active updating mechanism. Treat them as frozen baselines, not current figures.
2
The gender data surveillance gap is fundable
A proposal to establish a systematic gender outcomes monitoring function for India's health sector — replicating what CDC previously provided — is fundable by GIZ or FCDO right now. Frame it as filling a gap left by US withdrawal, not as creating something new. The framing matters enormously for bilateral funders justifying their India portfolio expansion.
3
Submit evidence to Parliamentary committees
The Standing Committee on WCD (which produced the March 25, 2026 report on Nirbhaya Fund) and the Rural Development Committee both rely on civil society evidence. With USAID-funded research organisations gone, there is an unoccupied space for organisations that can produce policy-relevant evidence and get it into parliamentary visibility.
